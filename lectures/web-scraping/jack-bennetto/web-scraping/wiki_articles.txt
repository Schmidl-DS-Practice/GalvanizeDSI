described since <a href=\"/wiki/Classical_antiquity\" title=\"Classical antiquity\">antiquity</a> as a beast with a single large, pointed, spiraling <a href=\"/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)\" title=\"Horn (anatomy)\">horn</a> projecting from its forehead. The unicorn was depicted in ancient seals of the <a href=\"/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Indus Valley Civilization\">Indus Valley Civilization</a> and was mentioned by the <a href=\"/wiki/Ancient_Greece\" title=\"Ancient Greece\">ancient Greeks</a> in accounts of <a href=\"/wiki/Natural_history\" title=\"Natural history\">natural history</a> by various writers, including <a href=\"/wiki/Ctesias\" title=\"Ctesias\">Ctesias</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Strabo\" title=\"Strabo\">Strabo</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger\" title=\"Pliny the Younger\">Pliny the Younger</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus\" title=\"Claudius Aelianus\">Aelian</a><sup id=\"cite_ref-Britannica_2-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Britannica-2\">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href=\"/wiki/Cosmas_Indicopleustes\" title=\"Cosmas Indicopleustes\">Cosmas Indicopleustes</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-3\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-3\">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href=\"/wiki/Bible\" title=\"Bible\">Bible</a> also describes an animal, the <a href=\"/wiki/Re%27em\" title=\"Re&#39;em\">re'em</a>, which some versions translate as <i>unicorn</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Britannica_2-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Britannica-2\">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In European folklore, the unicorn is often depicted as a white <a href=\"/wiki/Horse\" title=\"Horse\">horse</a>-like or <a href=\"/wiki/Goat\" title=\"Goat\">goat</a>-like animal with a long horn and cloven hooves (sometimes a goat's beard). In the <a href=\"/wiki/Middle_Ages\" title=\"Middle Ages\">Middle Ages</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Renaissance\" title=\"Renaissance\">Renaissance</a>, it was commonly described as an extremely wild <a href=\"/wiki/Forest\" title=\"Forest\">woodland</a> creature, a symbol of purity and grace, which could be captured only by a virgin. In the encyclopedias, its horn was said to have the power to render poisoned water potable and to heal sickness. In medieval and Renaissance times, the tusk of the <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a> was sometimes sold as unicorn horn.\n</p><p>Even in the twenty-first century, the unicorn holds a place in popular culture. href=\"/wiki/City_Hall,_Bristol\" title=\"City Hall, Bristol\">City Hall, Bristol</a></div></div></div>\n<p>A number of seals seemingly depicting unicorns have been found from the <a href=\"/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation\" title=\"Indus Valley Civilisation\">Indus Valley Civilisation</a>. <sup id=\"cite_ref-5\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-5\">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Seals with such a design are thought to be a mark of high <a href=\"/wiki/Social_rank\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Social rank\">social rank</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-6\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-6\">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These have also been interpreted as representations of <a href=\"/wiki/Aurochs\" title=\"Aurochs\">aurochs</a>\u2014a type of large wild cattle that formerly inhabited Europe, Asia and North Africa\u2014or derivatives of aurochs, because the animal is always shown in profile, indicating there may have supposed to have been another horn, which is not seen.<sup id=\"cite_ref-7\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-7\">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unicorns are not found in <a href=\"/wiki/Greek_mythology\" title=\"Greek mythology\">Greek mythology</a>, but rather in the accounts of <a href=\"/wiki/Natural_history\" title=\"Natural history\">natural history</a>, for Greek writers of natural history were convinced of the reality of unicorns, which they believed lived in India, a distant and fabulous realm for them. The earliest description is from <a href=\"/wiki/Ctesias\" title=\"Ctesias\">Ctesias</a>, who in his book <i><a href=\"/wiki/Indica_(Ctesias)\" title=\"Indica (Ctesias)\">Indika</a></i> (\"On <a href=\"/wiki/India\" title=\"India\">India</a>\") described them as <a href=\"/wiki/Onager\" title=\"Onager\">wild asses</a>, fleet of foot, having a horn a <a href=\"/wiki/Cubit\" title=\"Cubit\">cubit</a> and a half (700&#160;mm, 28 inches) in length, and colored white, red and black.<sup id=\"cite_ref-8\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-8\">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\"><div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Unicorn_in_Apadana,_Shush,_Iran--2017-10.jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg/220px-Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"165\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg/330px-Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg/440px-Unicorn_in_Apadana%2C_Shush%2C_Iran--2017-10.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"3755\" data-file-height=\"2821\" /></a>  <div class=\"thumbcaption\"><div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Unicorn_in_Apadana,_Shush,_Iran--2017-10.jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>Unicorn in <a href=\"/wiki/Apadana\" title=\"Apadana\">Apadana</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Susa\" title=\"Susa\">Shush</a>, Iran</div></div></div>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Ctesias\" title=\"Ctesias\">Ctesias</a> got his information while living in <a href=\"/wiki/Persian_Empire\" title=\"Persian Empire\">Persia</a>. Unicorns on a relief sculpture have been found at the ancient Persian capital of <a href=\"/wiki/Persepolis\" title=\"Persepolis\">Persepolis</a> in Iran.<sup id=\"cite_ref-9\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-9\">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  <a href=\"/wiki/Aristotle\" title=\"Aristotle\">Aristotle</a> must be following Ctesias when he mentions two one-horned animals, the <a href=\"/wiki/Oryx\" title=\"Oryx\">oryx</a> (a kind of <a href=\"/wiki/Antelope\" title=\"Antelope\">antelope</a>) and the so-called \"Indian ass\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-10\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-10\">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-11\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-11\">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href=\"/wiki/Strabo\" title=\"Strabo\">Strabo</a> says that in the <a href=\"/wiki/Caucasus\" title=\"Caucasus\">Caucasus</a> there were one-horned horses with stag-like heads.<sup id=\"cite_ref-12\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-12\">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n<a href=\"/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder\" title=\"Pliny the Elder\">Pliny the Elder</a> mentions the oryx and an Indian <a href=\"/wiki/Ox\" title=\"Ox\">ox</a> (perhaps a <a href=\"/wiki/Rhinoceros\" title=\"Rhinoceros\">rhinoceros</a>) as one-horned beasts, as well as \"a very fierce animal called the monoceros which has the head of the <a href=\"/wiki/Deer\" title=\"Deer\">stag</a>, the feet of the <a href=\"/wiki/Elephant\" title=\"Elephant\">elephant</a>, and the tail of the <a href=\"/wiki/Boar\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Boar\">boar</a>, while the rest of the body is like that of the horse; it makes a deep lowing noise, and has a single black horn, which projects from the middle of its forehead, two cubits [900&#160;mm, 35 inches] in length.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-13\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-13\">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In <i>On the Nature of Animals</i> (<i>\u03a0\u03b5\u03c1\u1f76 \u0396\u1ff4\u03c9\u03bd \u1f38\u03b4\u03b9\u03cc\u03c4\u03b7\u03c4\u03bf\u03c2</i>, <i>De natura animalium</i>), <a href=\"/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus\" title=\"Claudius Aelianus\">Aelian</a>, quoting Ctesias, adds that India produces also a one-horned horse (iii. 41; iv. 52),<sup id=\"cite_ref-14\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-14\">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-15\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-15\">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and says (xvi. 20)<sup id=\"cite_ref-16\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-16\">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> that the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Monoceros_(legendary_creature)\" title=\"Monoceros (legendary creature)\">monoceros</a></i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Greek_language\" title=\"Greek language\">Greek</a>: <span lang=\"el\">\u03bc\u03bf\u03bd\u03cc\u03ba\u03b5\u03c1\u03c9\u03c2</span>) was sometimes called <i>cartazonos</i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Greek_language\" title=\"Greek language\">Greek</a>: <span lang=\"el\">\u03ba\u03b1\u03c1\u03c4\u03ac\u03b6\u03c9\u03bd\u03bf\u03c2</span>), which may be a form of the Arabic <i><a href=\"/wiki/Karkadann\" title=\"Karkadann\">karkadann</a></i>, meaning \"<a href=\"/wiki/Rhinoceros\" title=\"Rhinoceros\">rhinoceros</a>\".\n</p><p><a href=\"/wiki/Cosmas_Indicopleustes\" title=\"Cosmas Indicopleustes\">Cosmas Indicopleustes</a>, a merchant of <a href=\"/wiki/Alexandria\" title=\"Alexandria\">Alexandria</a> who lived in the 6th century, made a voyage to India and subsequently wrote works on <a href=\"/wiki/Cosmography\" title=\"Cosmography\">cosmography</a>. He gives a description of a unicorn based on four brass figures in the palace of the King of <a href=\"/wiki/Ethiopia\" title=\"Ethiopia\">Ethiopia</a>. He states, from report, that \"it is impossible to take this ferocious beast alive; and that all its strength lies in its horn. When it finds itself pursued and in danger of capture, it throws itself from a precipice, and turns so aptly in falling, that it receives all the shock upon the horn, and so escapes safe and sound\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-17\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-17\">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-18\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"/wiki/Basel_Historical_Museum\" title=\"Basel Historical Museum\">Basel Historical Museum</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p><a href=\"/wiki/Middle_Ages\" title=\"Middle Ages\">Medieval</a> knowledge of the fabulous beast stemmed from <a href=\"/wiki/Re%27em\" title=\"Re&#39;em\">biblical</a> and ancient sources, and the creature was variously represented as a kind of <a href=\"/wiki/Onager\" title=\"Onager\">wild ass</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Goat\" title=\"Goat\">goat</a>, or <a href=\"/wiki/Horse\" title=\"Horse\">horse</a>.\n</p><p>The predecessor of the medieval <a href=\"/wiki/Bestiary\" title=\"Bestiary\">bestiary</a>, compiled in <a href=\"/wiki/Late_Antiquity\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Late Antiquity\">Late Antiquity</a> and known as <i><a href=\"/wiki/Physiologus\" title=\"Physiologus\">Physiologus</a></i> (<i>\u03a6\u03c5\u03c3\u03b9\u03bf\u03bb\u03cc\u03b3\u03bf\u03c2</i>), popularized an elaborate <a href=\"/wiki/Allegory\" title=\"Allegory\">allegory</a> in which a unicorn, trapped by a maiden (representing the <a href=\"/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mary (mother of Jesus)\">Virgin Mary</a>), stood for the <a href=\"/wiki/Incarnation\" title=\"Incarnation\">Incarnation</a>. As soon as the unicorn sees her, it lays its head on her lap and falls asleep. This became a basic emblematic tag that underlies medieval notions of the unicorn, justifying its appearance in every form of <a href=\"/wiki/Sacred_art\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Sacred art\">religious art.</a> Interpretations of the unicorn myth focus on the medieval lore of beguiled lovers,<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">&#91;<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)\">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> whereas some religious writers interpret the unicorn and its death as the <a href=\"/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Passion (Christianity)\">Passion of Christ</a>. The myths refer to a beast with one horn that can only be tamed by a <a href=\"/wiki/Virginity\" title=\"Virginity\">virgin</a>; subsequently, some writers translated this into an allegory for Christ's relationship with the Virgin Mary.\n</p><p>The unicorn also figured in <a href=\"/wiki/Courtly_love\" title=\"Courtly love\">courtly terms</a>: for some 13th-century <a href=\"/wiki/France\" title=\"France\">French</a> authors such as <a href=\"/wiki/Theobald_I_of_Navarre\" title=\"Theobald I of Navarre\">Thibaut of Champagne</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Richard_de_Fournival\" title=\"Richard de Fournival\">Richard de Fournival</a>, the lover is attracted to his lady as the unicorn is to the virgin. With the rise of <a href=\"/wiki/Renaissance_humanism\" title=\"Renaissance humanism\">humanism</a>, the unicorn also acquired more orthodox secular meanings, emblematic of chaste love and faithful marriage. It plays this role in <a href=\"/wiki/Petrarch\" title=\"Petrarch\">Petrarch</a>'s <i>Triumph of Chastity</i>, and on the reverse of <a href=\"/wiki/Piero_della_Francesca\" title=\"Piero della Francesca\">Piero della Francesca</a>'s portrait of Battista Strozzi, paired with that of her husband <a href=\"/wiki/Federico_da_Montefeltro\" title=\"Federico da Montefeltro\">Federico da Montefeltro</a> (painted <i>c</i> 1472-74), Bianca's triumphal car is drawn by a pair of unicorns.<sup id=\"cite_ref-19\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-19\">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href=\"/wiki/Throne_Chair_of_Denmark\" title=\"Throne Chair of Denmark\">Throne Chair of Denmark</a> is made of \"unicorn horns\" \u2013 almost certainly <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhal</a> tusks. The same material was used for ceremonial cups because the unicorn's horn continued to be believed to neutralize poison, following classical authors.\n</p><p>The unicorn, tamable only by a virgin woman, was well established in medieval lore by the time <a href=\"/wiki/Marco_Polo\" title=\"Marco Polo\">Marco Polo</a> described them as \"scarcely smaller than elephants. They have the hair of a buffalo and feet like an elephant's. They have a single large black horn in the middle of the forehead... They have a head like a wild boar's\u2026 They spend their time by preference wallowing in <a href=\"/wiki/Mud\" title=\"Mud\">mud</a> and slime. They are very ugly brutes to look at. They are not at all such as we describe them when we relate that they let themselves be captured by virgins, but clean contrary to our notions.\" It is clear that Marco Polo was describing a rhinoceros.<sup id=\"cite_ref-20\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-20\">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class=\"thumb tleft\"><div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:152px;\"><a title=\"Unicorn horn\">Unicorn horn</a></div>\n<p>The horn itself and the substance it was made of was called <b>alicorn</b>, and it was believed that the horn holds magical and medicinal properties. The <a href=\"/wiki/Danish_people\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Danish people\">Danish</a> physician <a href=\"/wiki/Ole_Worm\" title=\"Ole Worm\">Ole Worm</a> determined in 1638 that the alleged alicorns were the tusks of narwhals.<sup id=\"cite_ref-21\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-21\">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Such beliefs were examined wittily and at length in 1646 by Sir <a href=\"/wiki/Thomas_Browne\" title=\"Thomas Browne\">Thomas Browne</a> in his <i><a href=\"/wiki/Pseudodoxia_Epidemica\" title=\"Pseudodoxia Epidemica\">Pseudodoxia Epidemica</a></i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-22\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-22\">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>False alicorn powder, made from the tusks of <a href=\"/wiki/Narwhal\" title=\"Narwhal\">narwhals</a> or horns of various animals, has been sold in Europe for medicinal purposes as late as 1741.<sup id=\"cite_ref-23\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-23\">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The alicorn was thought to cure many diseases and have the ability to detect poisons, and many physicians would make \"cures\" and sell them. Cups were made from alicorn for kings and given as a gift; these were usually made of <a href=\"/wiki/Ivory\" title=\"Ivory\">ivory</a> or <a href=\"/wiki/Walrus\" title=\"Walrus\">walrus</a> ivory. Entire horns were very precious in the Middle Ages and were often really the tusks of narwhals.<sup id=\"cite_ref-24\" class=\"reference\"><a one of his notebooks <a href=\"/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci\" title=\"Leonardo da Vinci\">Leonardo da Vinci</a> wrote:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The unicorn, through its intemperance and not knowing how to control itself, for the love it bears to fair maidens forgets its ferocity and wildness; and laying aside all fear it will go up to a seated damsel and go to sleep in her lap, and thus the hunters take it.<sup id=\"cite_ref-25\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-25\">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The famous late <a href=\"/wiki/Gothic_art\" title=\"Gothic art\">Gothic</a> series of seven <a href=\"/wiki/Tapestry\" title=\"Tapestry\">tapestry</a> hangings <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Hunt_of_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Hunt of the Unicorn\">The Hunt of the Unicorn</a></i> are a high point in <a href=\"/wiki/Europe\" title=\"Europe\">European</a> tapestry manufacture, combining both secular and religious themes. The tapestries now hang in <a href=\"/wiki/The_Cloisters\" title=\"The Cloisters\">the Cloisters</a> division of the <a href=\"/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art\" title=\"Metropolitan Museum of Art\">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in <a href=\"/wiki/New_York_City\" title=\"New York City\">New York City</a>. In the series, richly dressed <a href=\"/wiki/Nobility\" title=\"Nobility\">noblemen</a>, accompanied by huntsmen and hounds, pursue a unicorn against <i><a href=\"/wiki/Mille-fleur\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Mille-fleur\">mille-fleur</a></i> backgrounds or settings of buildings and gardens. They bring the animal to bay with the help of a maiden who traps it with her charms, appear to kill it, and bring it back to a castle; in the last and most famous panel, \"The Unicorn in Captivity\", the unicorn is shown alive again and happy, chained to a <a href=\"/wiki/Pomegranate\" title=\"Pomegranate\">pomegranate</a> tree surrounded by a fence, in a field of flowers. Scholars conjecture that the red stains on its flanks are not blood but rather the juice from pomegranates, which were a symbol of fertility. However, the true meaning of the mysterious resurrected unicorn in the last panel is unclear. The series was woven about 1500 in the <a href=\"/wiki/Low_Countries\" title=\"Low Countries\">Low Countries</a>, probably <a href=\"/wiki/Brussels\" title=\"Brussels\">Brussels</a> or <a href=\"/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Li\u00e8ge (city)\">Li\u00e8ge</a>, for an unknown patron. A set of six <a href=\"/wiki/Engraving\" title=\"Engraving\">engravings</a> on the same theme, treated rather differently, were engraved by the French artist <a href=\"/wiki/Jean_Duvet\" title=\"Jean Duvet\">Jean Duvet</a> in the 1540s.\n</p><p>Another famous set of six tapestries of <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Lady_and_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Lady and the Unicorn\">Dame \u00e0 la licorne</a></i> (\"Lady with the unicorn\") in the <a href=\"/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_Cluny\" title=\"Mus\u00e9e de Cluny\">Mus\u00e9e de Cluny</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Paris\" title=\"Paris\">Paris</a>, were also woven in the <a href=\"/wiki/Southern_Netherlands\" title=\"Southern Netherlands\">Southern Netherlands</a> before 1500, and show the five senses (the gateways to temptation) and finally Love (\"A mon seul desir\" the legend reads), with unicorns featured in each piece. Facsimiles of these unicorn tapestries were woven for permanent display in <a href=\"/wiki/Stirling_Castle\" title=\"Stirling Castle\">Stirling Castle</a>, <a href=\"/wiki/Scotland\" title=\"Scotland\">Scotland</a>, to take the place of a set recorded in the castle in a <a href=\"/wiki/Scottish_Royal_tapestry_collection\" title=\"Scottish Royal tapestry collection\">16th-century inventory</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-26\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-26\">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A rather rare, late-15th-century, variant depiction of the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Hortus_conclusus\" title=\"Hortus conclusus\">hortus conclusus</a></i> in religious art combined the <a href=\"/wiki/Annunciation_to_Mary\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Annunciation to Mary\">Annunciation to Mary</a> with the themes of the <i>Hunt of the Unicorn</i> and <i>Virgin and Unicorn</i>, so popular in secular art. The unicorn already functioned as a symbol of the <a href=\"/wiki/Incarnation_of_Christ\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Incarnation of Christ\">Incarnation</a> and whether this meaning is intended in many <i>prima facie</i> secular depictions can be a difficult matter of scholarly interpretation. There is no such ambiguity in the scenes where the archangel <a href=\"/wiki/Gabriel\" title=\"Gabriel\">Gabriel</a> is shown blowing a horn, as hounds chase the unicorn into the Virgin's arms, and a little Christ Child descends on rays of light from God the Father. The <a href=\"/wiki/Council_of_Trent\" title=\"Council of Trent\">Council of Trent</a> finally banned this somewhat over-elaborated, if charming, depiction,<sup id=\"cite_ref-27\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-27\">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> partly on the grounds of realism, as no one now believed the unicorn to be a real animal.\n</p><p><a href=\"/wiki/Shakespeare\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shakespeare\">Shakespeare</a> scholars describe unicorns being captured by a hunter standing in front of a tree, the unicorn goaded into charging; the hunter would step aside the last moment and the unicorn would embed its horn deeply into the tree (See annotations<sup id=\"cite_ref-28\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-28\">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> of <a href=\"/wiki/Timon_of_Athens\" title=\"Timon of Athens\">Timon of Athens</a>, Act 4, scene 3, c. line 341: \"wert thou the unicorn, pride and wrath would confound thee and make thine own self the conquest of thy fury\".)\n</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Heraldry\">Heraldry</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6\" title=\"Edit section: Heraldry\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href=\"/wiki/Heraldry\" title=\"Heraldry\">heraldry</a>, a unicorn is often depicted as a horse with a goat's cloven hooves and beard, a lion's tail, and a slender, spiral horn on its forehead<sup id=\"cite_ref-friar_29-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-friar-29\">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> (non-equine attributes may be replaced with equine ones, as can be seen from the following gallery). Whether because it was an emblem of the Incarnation or of the fearsome animal passions of raw nature, the unicorn was not widely used in early heraldry, but became popular from the 15th century.<sup id=\"cite_ref-friar_29-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-friar-29\">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Though sometimes shown collared and chained, which may be taken as an indication that it has been tamed or tempered, it is more usually shown collared with a broken chain attached, showing that it has broken free from its bondage.\n</p>\n<h4><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Scotland\">Scotland</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7\" title=\"Edit section: Scotland\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role=\"note\" class=\"hatnote navigation-not-searchable\">See also: <a href=\"/wiki/The_Lion_and_the_Unicorn\" title=\"The Lion and the Unicorn\">The Lion and the Unicorn</a></div>\n<p>In heraldry the unicorn is best known as a symbol of <a href=\"/wiki/Scotland\" title=\"Scotland\">Scotland</a>: the Unicorn was believed to be the natural enemy of the lion - a symbol that the English royals adopted around a hundred years before<sup id=\"cite_ref-30\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-30\">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and was also chosen because it was seen as a proud and haughty beast which would rather die than be captured, just as Scots would fight to remain sovereign and unconquered.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Nisbet_31-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Nisbet-31\">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Two unicorns supported the <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_arms_of_Scotland\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Royal arms of Scotland\">royal arms</a> of the King of Scots, and since the <a href=\"/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707\" title=\"Acts of Union 1707\">1707 union</a> of England and Scotland, the <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_arms_of_the_United_Kingdom\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Royal arms of the United Kingdom\">royal arms of the United Kingdom</a> have been supported by a unicorn along with an English lion. Two versions of the royal arms exist: that used in Scotland gives more emphasis to the Scottish elements, placing the unicorn on the left and giving it a crown, whereas the version used in England and elsewhere gives the English elements more prominence.\n</p><p>Golden coins known as the <a href=\"/wiki/Unicorn_(coin)\" title=\"Unicorn (coin)\">unicorn</a> and half-unicorn, both with a unicorn on the <a href=\"/wiki/Obverse\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Obverse\">obverse</a>, were used in Scotland in the 15th and 16th century. In the same realm, carved unicorns were often used as finials on the pillars of <a href=\"/wiki/Mercat_cross\" title=\"Mercat cross\">Mercat crosses</a>, and denoted that the settlement was a <a href=\"/wiki/Royal_burgh\" title=\"Royal burgh\">royal burgh</a>. Certain noblemen such as the <a href=\"/wiki/Earl_of_Kinnoull\" title=\"Earl of Kinnoull\">Earl of Kinnoull</a> were given special permission to use the unicorn in their arms, as an <a href=\"/wiki/Augmentation_of_honour\" title=\"Augmentation of honour\">augmentation of honour</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-Nisbet_31-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-Nisbet-31\">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>   The crest for <a href=\"/wiki/Clan_Cunningham\" title=\"Clan Cunningham\">Clan Cunningham</a> bears a unicorn head.<sup id=\"cite_ref-32\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-32\">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Gallery\">Gallery</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span animal called the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Re%E2%80%99em\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Re\u2019em\">re\u2019em</a></i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Hebrew_language\" title=\"Hebrew language\">Hebrew</a>: <span lang=\"he\" dir=\"rtl\">\u05e8\u05b0\u05d0\u05b5\u05dd</span>&#8206;) is mentioned in several places in the <a href=\"/wiki/Hebrew_Bible\" title=\"Hebrew Bible\">Hebrew Bible</a>, often as a metaphor representing strength. \"The allusions to the <i>re'em</i> as a wild, un-tamable animal of great strength and agility, with mighty horn or horns (Job xxxix. 9\u201312; Ps. xxii. 21, xxix. 6; Num. xxiii. 22, xxiv. 8; Deut. xxxiii. 17; comp. Ps. xcii. 11), best fit the <a href=\"/wiki/Aurochs\" title=\"Aurochs\">aurochs</a> (<i>Bos primigenius</i>). This view is supported by the Assyrian <i>rimu,</i> which is often used as a metaphor of strength, and is depicted as a powerful, fierce, wild mountain bull with large horns.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-33\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-33\">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This animal was often depicted in ancient <a href=\"/wiki/Mesopotamia\" title=\"Mesopotamia\">Mesopotamian</a> art in profile, with only one horn visible.\n</p><p>The translators of the <a href=\"/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Authorized King James Version\">Authorized King James Version</a> of the <a href=\"/wiki/Bible\" title=\"Bible\">Bible</a> (1611) followed the Greek <a href=\"/wiki/Septuagint\" title=\"Septuagint\">Septuagint</a> (<i>monokeros</i>) and the Latin <a href=\"/wiki/Vulgate\" title=\"Vulgate\">Vulgate</a> (<i>unicornis</i>)<sup id=\"cite_ref-34\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-34\">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and employed <i>unicorn</i> to translate <i>re'em</i>, providing a recognizable animal that was proverbial for its un-tamable nature. The <a href=\"/wiki/American_Standard_Version\" title=\"American Standard Version\">American Standard Version</a> translates this term \"wild ox\" in each case.\n</p>\n<ul><li>\"God brought them out of Egypt; he hath as it were the strength of an <b>unicorn</b>.\"\u2014<a rel=\"nofollow\" class=\"external text\" href=\"http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Numbers+23:22\u201323:22&amp;version=nrsv\">Numbers 23:22</a></li>\n<li>\"God brought him forth out of Egypt; he hath as it were the strength of an <b>unicorn</b>.\"\u2014<a rel=\"nofollow\" class=\"external text\" href=\"http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Numbers+24:8\u201324:8&amp;version=nrsv\">Numbers 24:8</a></li>\n<li>\"His glory is like the firstling of his bullock, and his horns are like the horns of <b>unicorns</b>: with them he shall push the people together to the ends of the earth.\"\u2014<a rel=\"nofollow\" class=\"external text\" href=\"http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Deuteronomy+33:17\u201333:17&amp;version=nrsv\">Deuteronomy 33:17</a></li>\n<li>\"Will the <b>unicorn</b> be willing to serve thee, or abide by thy crib? However, some rabbis in the <a href=\"/wiki/Talmud\" title=\"Talmud\">Talmud</a> debate the proposition that the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Tahash\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Tahash\">Tahash</a></i> animal (Exodus 25, 26, 35, 36 and 39; Numbers 4; and Ezekiel 16:10) was a domestic, single-horned <a href=\"/wiki/Kosher\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Kosher\">kosher</a> creature that existed in Moses' time, or that it was similar to the <i>keresh</i> animal described in <a href=\"/wiki/Morris_Jastrow\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Morris Jastrow\">Morris Jastrow</a>'s Talmudic dictionary as \"a kind of antelope, unicorn\".<sup id=\"cite_ref-35\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-35\">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Chinese_mythology\">Chinese mythology</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Unicorn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11\" title=\"Edit section: Chinese mythology\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class=\"thumb tright\"><div class=\"thumbinner\" style=\"width:222px;\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_(386-534_CE).jpg\" class=\"image\"><img alt=\"\" src=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/220px-Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg\" decoding=\"async\" width=\"220\" height=\"165\" class=\"thumbimage\" srcset=\"//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/330px-Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg/440px-Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_%28386-534_CE%29.jpg 2x\" data-file-width=\"1024\" data-file-height=\"768\" /></a>  <div class=\"thumbcaption\"><div class=\"magnify\"><a href=\"/wiki/File:Pottery_unicorn._Northern_Wei_(386-534_CE).jpg\" class=\"internal\" title=\"Enlarge\"></a></div>Pottery Unicorn. Northern Wei. Shaanxi History Museum</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i><a href=\"/wiki/Qilin\" title=\"Qilin\">qilin</a></i> (<a href=\"/wiki/Chinese_language\" title=\"Chinese language\">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang=\"zh\">\u9e92\u9e9f</span>), a creature in <a href=\"/wiki/Chinese_mythology\" title=\"Chinese mythology\">Chinese mythology</a>, is sometimes called \"the Chinese unicorn\", and some ancient accounts describe a single horn as its defining feature. However, it is more accurately described as a <a href=\"/wiki/Category:Mythological_hybrids\" title=\"Category:Mythological hybrids\">hybrid animal</a> that looks less unicorn than <a href=\"/wiki/Chimera_(mythology)\" title=\"Chimera (mythology)\">chimera</a>, with the body of a deer, the head of a lion, green <a href=\"/wiki/Scale_(zoology)\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Scale (zoology)\">scales</a> and a long forwardly-curved horn. The <a href=\"/wiki/Japanese_mythology\" title=\"Japanese mythology\">Japanese</a> version (<i>kirin</i>) more closely resembles the Western unicorn, even though it is based on the Chinese <i>qilin</i>. The Qu\u1ebb Ly of <a href=\"/wiki/Vietnam\" title=\"Vietnam\">Vietnamese</a> myth, similarly sometimes mistranslated \"unicorn\" is a symbol of wealth and prosperity that made its first appearance during the Duong Dynasty, about 600 CE, to Emperor Duong Cao To, after a military victory which resulted in his conquest of <a href=\"/wiki/T%C3%A2y_Nguy%C3%AAn\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"T\u00e2y Nguy\u00ean\">T\u00e2y Nguy\u00ean</a>. In November 2012 the History Institute of the DPRK Academy of Social Sciences, as well as the <a href=\"/wiki/Korean_Central_News_Agency\" title=\"Korean Central News Agency\">Korea News Service</a>, reported that the <a href=\"/wiki/Kiringul\" title=\"Kiringul\">Kiringul</a> had been found, which is associated with a kirin ridden by <a href=\"/wiki/King_Dongmyeong_of_Goguryeo\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"King Dongmyeong of Goguryeo\">King Dongmyeong of Goguryeo</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-36\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-36\">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-37\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-37\">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beginning in the <a href=\"/wiki/Ming_Dynasty\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Ming Dynasty\">Ming Dynasty</a>, the <i>qilin</i> became associated with <a href=\"/wiki/Giraffe\" title=\"Giraffe\">giraffes</a>, after <a href=\"/wiki/Zheng_He\" title=\"Zheng He\">Zheng He</a>'s <a href=\"/wiki/Treasure_voyages\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Treasure voyages\">voyage</a> to <a href=\"/wiki/East_Africa\" title=\"East Africa\">East Africa</a> brought a pair of the long-necked animals and introduced them at court in <a href=\"/wiki/Nanjing\" title=\"Nanjing\">Nanjing</a> as <i>qilin</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-38\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-38\">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The resemblance to the <i>qilin</i> was noted in the giraffe's <a href=\"/wiki/Ossicones\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Ossicones\">ossicones</a> (bony protrusions from the skull resembling horns), graceful movements, and peaceful demeanor.<sup id=\"cite_ref-39\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-39\">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href=\"/wiki/Shanhaijing\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shanhaijing\">Shanhaijing</a> (117) also mentioned <i>Bo</i>-horse (<a href=\"/wiki/Chinese_language\" title=\"Chinese language\">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang=\"zh\">\u99ee\u99ac</span>; <a href=\"/wiki/Pinyin\" title=\"Pinyin\">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang=\"zh-Latn-pinyin\">b\u00f3m\u01ce</span></i>), a chimera horse with ox tail, single horn, white body, and its sound like person calling. The creature is lived at Honest-head Mountain. <a href=\"/wiki/Guo_Pu\" title=\"Guo Pu\">Guo Pu</a> in his <i>jiangfu</i> said that <i>Bo</i>-horse able to walk on water. Another similar creature also mentioned in <a href=\"/wiki/Shanhaijing\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Shanhaijing\">Shanhaijing</a> (80) to live in Mount Winding-Centre as <i>Bo</i> (<a lang=\"zh-Latn-pinyin\">b\u00f3</span></i>), but with black tail, tiger's teeth and claws, and also devour leopards and tigers.<sup id=\"cite_ref-str_40-0\" class=\"reference\"><a
